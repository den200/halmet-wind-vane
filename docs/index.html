The problem

A healthy sensor trapped behind a dead screen

Raymarine's ST60+ wind instrument is two separate things on one cable: a masthead transducer up the mast, and a display head at the helm. On this boat the display head failed — but the masthead, 15 m up, was still perfectly alive, spinning and pointing. Replacing it means a mast climb and a whole new instrument system. The masthead just needed something new to listen to it.

✗ Dead

The ST60+ display head

The helm display gave up. With it went the only thing that knew how to decode the masthead — and the only path wind had onto the boat's electronics.

✓ Alive

The masthead transducer

A sine/cosine resolver for wind angle and a cup anemometer pulsing for wind speed. Healthy, calibrated by years aloft, and speaking a simple analog language — if you know how to read it.

The modern half of the boat: an Orca Core 2 and the rest of the electronics live on a NMEA 2000 backbone — which never spoke ST60+. The job: bridge the old masthead to the new bus.

How it works

From an analog resolver to a digital PGN, in two pipelines

The HALMET board taps three wires off the masthead. Two carry the sin/cos voltages that encode the vane angle; one pulses with the cups. The firmware turns them into calibrated apparent wind and puts it on the wire as PGN 130306, ten times a second.

Why sin/cos & atan2? The masthead encodes angle as two voltages — one tracks the sine of the vane angle, the other the cosine. Recovering the angle is just atan2(sin, cos). Because atan2 is scale-invariant, calibration only has to centre each channel (subtract its mid-voltage); a magnitude guard reports "no data" instead of a stuck angle if the masthead falls silent.

The hardware

Three signal wires, one buck, one bus tap

The Hat Labs HALMET is purpose-built for exactly this: ±32 V-tolerant Schmitt-triggered digital inputs, an external 16-bit ADS1115 on the analog inputs, and the ESP32's built-in CAN (TWAI) for NMEA 2000. It draws its own power from the backbone. The masthead's 8 V comes from a small external buck — the one part you must set before wiring anything.

  • Board: HALMET (ESP32-WROOM-32E, TWAI CAN @ 250 kbit/s)
  • Analog: ADS1115 16-bit ADC, fixed 10:1 input divider
  • Pulse: hardware Schmitt trigger, no debounce needed
  • Masthead power: external MP1584 buck set to 8.0 V
  • Bus: powered from N2K; on-board 120 Ω terminator left open

The software

SensESP v3, and calibration you never reflash for

Built on SensESP v3 (PlatformIO / pioarduino) with the NMEA2000 library on the ESP32 TWAI driver. Every tunable — angle zero, direction, channel centres, the speed constant — is a live web-UI field at http://halmet-wind.local/, persisted to flash. You calibrate by sailing, not by recompiling.

🧭

Angle, aligned live

Set the zero to the bow and flip direction with a single sin_sign field if the wind reads backward — no wire-swapping up the mast.

🌀

Speed, by ellipse-fit

With the mast up, the sin/cos centres come from an ellipse fit on logged free-rotation data; speed K is trimmed against GPS in calm air.

📡

One source of truth

Apparent wind only — true wind is left for the chartplotter or SignalK to derive. No feedback loops, one authoritative PGN 130306.

// PGN 130306 every 100 ms from the latched, calibrated values.
// Angle is converted from SignalK's −π..+π to N2K's 0..2π; NaN → N2kDoubleNA.
event_loop()->onRepeat(100, []() {
  tN2kMsg msg;
  double awa = isfinite(last_awa_rad)
      ? (last_awa_rad < 0 ? last_awa_rad + 2*M_PI : last_awa_rad)
      : N2kDoubleNA;
  double aws = isfinite(last_aws_mps) ? last_aws_mps : N2kDoubleNA;
  SetN2kWindSpeed(msg, sid, aws, awa, N2kWind_Apparent);
  nmea2000->SendMsg(msg);   // → onto the backbone
});

The outcome

Apparent wind, back on the boat — from a "dead" instrument

What works

  • PGN 130306 on the backbone at a steady 10 Hz, source address 35
  • Apparent wind angle & speed on the Orca Core 2 and any N2K display
  • Same values on SignalK over WiFi for dashboards & logging
  • All four calibration parameters tunable live in the browser
  • Bench-validated, then proven on the water

Bill of the idea

No mast climb. No replacement masthead. No proprietary display. A ~€60 board, a buck converter, three wires off an existing cable, and open firmware — turning a decade-old transducer into a first-class citizen of a modern NMEA 2000 network.
