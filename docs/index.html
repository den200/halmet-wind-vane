The problem

A healthy sensor trapped behind a dead screen

The old Raymarine ST60+ wind instrument is really two things sharing one cable: a sensor at the top of the mast, and a display at the helm that knows how to read it. On this boat the display died — but the sensor, 15 m up, was perfectly fine, still pointing and spinning. Replacing it would mean a trip up the mast and a whole new set of instruments. It just needed something new to listen to it.

✗ Dead

The ST60+ display head

The helm screen gave up. And because it was the only thing that knew how to read the mast sensor, the boat lost its wind readings completely.

✓ Alive

The wind vane & cups

A vane that swings to point into the wind (that's the direction) and little cups that spin faster as it blows harder (that's the speed). Old, sturdy, still accurate — and speaking a simple electrical language, if you know how to listen.

The modern half of the boat: an Orca Core 2 chartplotter and the rest of the gear all talk over a shared digital network called NMEA 2000 — a language the old ST60+ never spoke. The whole job was to translate between the two.

How it works

Turning the old sensor's signals into a message the boat understands

The board taps just three wires from the existing mast cable. Two of them describe the wind angle; the third ticks along with the spinning cups. From those, the firmware works out the wind's direction and speed and announces them on the boat's network — ten times every second.

Why two voltages for one angle? The vane doesn't report its angle as a single number. Instead it sends two voltages that rise and fall as it turns — a bit like how the length and direction of a sundial's shadow together tell you the time. Comparing the two pins down exactly which way the vane is pointing. A neat bonus: if the sensor ever falls silent, the board notices and reports "no reading" rather than a frozen, misleading angle.

The hardware

Three signal wires, one buck, one bus tap

The Hat Labs HALMET is a small board made for exactly this kind of job. It shrugs off the rough, noisy voltages you find on a boat, reads the sensor with a precise voltmeter chip, and plugs straight into the NMEA 2000 network — drawing its own power from it, too. The only extra part is a small adjustable power supply that gives the mast sensor its 8 volts — and that's the one thing you must set before wiring anything.

  • A small HALMET board — a tiny computer with the boat-network connection built in
  • Reads the wind angle with a precise 16-bit voltmeter chip
  • Counts the speed pulses in hardware — rock-steady, no false ticks
  • Powers the mast sensor from a small adjustable supply set to 8.0 V
  • Runs off the boat network itself — no separate power supply needed

The software

Tune it from a browser — no laptop, no re-flashing

The firmware is built on SensESP, an open-source toolkit for boat sensors. Every adjustment — which way is "straight ahead", the direction of rotation, the speed scale — is a simple field on a web page the board hosts itself at http://halmet-wind.local/. You fine-tune it by sailing and typing numbers into a browser, never by plugging in a computer.

🧭

Point it at the bow

Tell it which way is forward, and flip the rotation with one setting if the wind ever reads backwards — no rewiring up the mast.

🌀

Calibrate by sailing

With the mast up, the angle is dialled in from readings logged while the vane swings freely; the speed is checked against your GPS in calm air.

📡

One honest source

It reports only the wind you actually feel on deck, and leaves "true wind" for the chartplotter to work out — so there's one clear source and no confusing duplicates.

For the curious, the core is tiny — ten times a second it wraps up the latest reading and drops it onto the network:

// Every 100 ms: package the latest calibrated wind reading and send it.
// Angle is converted from SignalK's −π..+π to N2K's 0..2π; NaN → N2kDoubleNA.
event_loop()->onRepeat(100, []() {
  tN2kMsg msg;
  double awa = isfinite(last_awa_rad)
      ? (last_awa_rad < 0 ? last_awa_rad + 2*M_PI : last_awa_rad)
      : N2kDoubleNA;
  double aws = isfinite(last_aws_mps) ? last_aws_mps : N2kDoubleNA;
  SetN2kWindSpeed(msg, sid, aws, awa, N2kWind_Apparent);
  nmea2000->SendMsg(msg);   // → onto the backbone
});

The outcome

Apparent wind, back on the boat — from a "dead" instrument

What works

  • Wind on the boat's network at a steady 10 readings a second
  • Direction & speed showing on the Orca Core 2 and any other display
  • The same readings also land in SignalK over WiFi — ready for any SignalK app or plugin (gauges like KIP, dashboards, loggers) you install
  • Every adjustment tweakable live in a browser — no laptop aboard
  • Tested on the bench, then proven out on the water

Bill of the idea

No mast climb. No replacement sensor. No pricey proprietary display. Just a ~€60 board, a small power supply, three wires off the existing cable, and open-source firmware — giving a decade-old wind sensor a full second life on a modern boat network.
